# Diagnosing and repairing transport failure in diabetes mortality prediction across population, hospital, and ICU databases

## Abstract

Transported clinical AI models may retain risk ranking while losing usable probabilities. We evaluated diabetes mortality model transport across NHANES, MIMIC-IV, and eICU in three directions: NHANES-to-MIMIC-IV as an extreme cross-setting stress test and bidirectional MIMIC-IV ICU-eICU as realistic ICU deployment. Using unweighted logistic regression as the primary analysis, transported models retained moderate discrimination (AUC 0.674-0.737), but expected calibration error (ECE; lower is better) and calibration slope diverged. NHANES-to-MIMIC-IV and MIMIC-IV ICU-to-eICU showed slope distortion, whereas eICU-to-MIMIC-IV ICU remained near calibrated despite strong source separability (ECE 0.016). Because endpoints differed, cross-scenario interpretation focused on failure mode and recalibration behavior, not absolute performance. Together, the cases operationally decoupled source-shift detection from probability-scale diagnosis: source separability identified shift but did not determine recalibration need. In the two slope-distorted directions, 100-event Platt updating reduced ECE below 0.02; the near-calibrated ICU direction required little updating.

Keywords: clinical AI; transportability; calibration; recalibration; dataset shift; diabetes; mortality prediction; MIMIC-IV; eICU; NHANES

## Introduction

Clinical prediction models are increasingly expected to travel across data environments. A model may be derived in a cohort study, population survey, registry, electronic health record system, or intensive-care database, but later be evaluated or deployed in a setting with different patients, measurements, outcome ascertainment, and baseline risks. Transparent reporting and evaluation guidance for prediction models now treats external validation, model updating, and deployment context as core concerns [1-3]. Large EHR and individual participant data resources create new opportunities for external validation, yet they also expose heterogeneity that can be hidden by a single pooled discrimination statistic [4-7]. For models intended to guide care, the central question is not only whether risk ranking is preserved, but whether predicted probabilities remain reliable in the target setting.

Recent clinical AI experience has made this problem concrete. External validation of a widely implemented proprietary sepsis prediction model found substantially lower discrimination than previously reported, with a hospitalization-level AUC of 0.63 rather than the developer-reported range of 0.76-0.83 [8]. Temporal validation studies in EHR-derived acute kidney injury and hospital mortality models have similarly shown that discrimination can remain relatively stable while calibration deteriorates as case mix, outcome prevalence, and care processes change [9,10]. Cross-site evaluations of clinical machine-learning systems have also shown that models can learn source-specific patterns, measurement practices, and local care processes that do not transport cleanly to new settings [11-15]. These studies show that external performance failure is not a rare technical inconvenience; it is a routine deployment problem.

Calibration is particularly important because many clinical actions are threshold based. A model with acceptable discrimination can still produce probabilities that are systematically too high, too low, or too compressed for the target population, distorting decisions even when rank ordering is partly preserved. Methodological work therefore recommends reporting calibration-in-the-large, calibration slope, and calibration curves alongside discrimination, and updating models when calibration is poor [7,16,17]. Simple updating strategies include intercept-only recalibration, logistic recalibration of the intercept and slope, local adaptation of existing models, and flexible nonparametric methods such as isotonic regression [18-23]. However, transported models do not all fail in the same way. Some models mainly require correction for baseline-risk displacement, whereas others require slope correction because the predicted risk range is distorted. Distinguishing these failure modes may be as important as measuring the average calibration error itself.

Diabetes mortality prediction provides a useful setting for studying this problem because diabetes is common, clinically heterogeneous, and associated with excess all-cause and cardiovascular mortality [24,25]. We therefore used a three-source design in which a population survey, a single-system hospital and ICU EHR, and a multicenter ICU database created complementary transport problems rather than one isolated benchmark. First, we transported a one-year mortality model from NHANES to MIMIC-IV, representing an extreme shift from a community population survey to a hospital EHR target. Second, we transported hospital mortality models between MIMIC-IV ICU and eICU in both directions, representing a more realistic ICU-to-ICU deployment scenario across institutions and measurement systems. These scenarios were not intended to represent the same endpoint; rather, they were designed as complementary deployment problems. The NHANES-to-MIMIC analysis tests cross-setting stress, whereas the MIMIC-IV ICU-eICU analyses test clinically harmonized ICU deployment. We asked whether discrimination survived transport, whether calibration failed through different mechanisms, and whether local recalibration with small numbers of target-site outcome events could repair calibration and decision utility. The core contribution is to treat source-shift detection and probability-scale diagnosis as separate deployment tasks: a source classifier can show that data environments are distinguishable, but calibration slope, intercept, and ECE determine whether the transported risk scale needs repair and which updater is appropriate.

## Results

### Source classifiers characterized separability but not transported calibration state

The analysis combined a population-survey cohort, a single-system hospital EHR cohort, and a multicenter ICU cohort. The NHANES one-year diabetes mortality cohort included 6,564 participants with known one-year outcome status and 166 deaths. The MIMIC-IV diabetes one-year mortality analysis included 133,450 admissions and 25,099 one-year death-composite events. For the ICU deployment scenario, the MIMIC-IV ICU diabetes cohort included 25,906 ICU admissions from 18,973 subjects, with 2,988 hospital deaths (11.5%). The eICU diabetes cohort included 55,486 stays with known hospital mortality status and 5,242 hospital deaths (9.4%). These hospital mortality rates supported ICU-to-ICU transport analysis, while the databases remained distinct clinical data-generating environments.

Source-classifier analyses confirmed strong separability between data sources. For the NHANES-MIMIC stress-test contrast, the random-forest source-classifier AUC was 0.812 with basic common features and 0.984 with laboratory-augmented features. For the MIMIC-IV ICU-eICU contrast, the random-forest source-classifier AUC was 0.784 with basic features, 0.878 after adding first-24-hour laboratory values, and 0.959 after adding ICU vital signs. The corresponding logistic-regression source-classifier AUCs were lower but showed the same pattern: 0.707 to 0.743 for NHANES-MIMIC and 0.628 to 0.748 for MIMIC-IV ICU-eICU. Within these three directions, these classifiers characterized source separability and suggested different origins of shift, but source-classifier AUC did not by itself specify the transported calibration state or recalibration need. NHANES-to-MIMIC mainly reflected sampling-frame and care-setting differences, whereas MIMIC-IV ICU-eICU more directly reflected ICU case mix, measurement processes, and institutional practice differences.

### Transported models retained moderate discrimination across all scenarios

Despite large source separability across the three transport directions, discrimination was partially preserved. This separation between rank performance and probability calibration motivated direct calibration diagnostics rather than reliance on AUC alone. Because the stress-test and ICU analyses used different endpoints, the following AUCs should be interpreted within their scenario rather than as endpoint-equivalent rankings. Transported unweighted logistic models retained moderate discrimination in all three primary transport directions (Table 3, which reports target evaluation sample size and events for each direction). In the extreme cross-setting stress test, the NHANES-to-MIMIC one-year mortality model achieved AUC 0.674 (95% CI 0.667-0.682) and PR AUC 0.319 (0.309-0.331). In the realistic ICU deployment scenario, the MIMIC-IV ICU-to-eICU hospital mortality model achieved AUC 0.707 (0.691-0.721) and PR AUC 0.234 (0.214-0.255). The reverse eICU-to-MIMIC-IV ICU model achieved AUC 0.737 (0.717-0.757) and PR AUC 0.293 (0.263-0.332).

This pattern was important because discrimination did not collapse even under large dataset shift. The three transported AUCs all fell within a relatively narrow range of 0.67-0.74, suggesting that part of the mortality risk ordering survived transport. However, retained discrimination did not guarantee a usable probability scale. NHANES-to-MIMIC had mean predicted risk 0.043 (0.043-0.044) versus observed event rate 0.190 (0.186-0.194), with ECE 0.146 (0.142-0.150). MIMIC-IV ICU-to-eICU had mean predicted risk 0.142 (0.139-0.145) versus event rate 0.090 (0.086-0.095), with ECE 0.059 (0.055-0.064). eICU-to-MIMIC-IV ICU was different: mean predicted risk was 0.100 (0.098-0.102) versus event rate 0.111 (0.104-0.119), and ECE was only 0.016 (0.013-0.026). Equal-width 10-bin ECEs were similar at 0.146, 0.052, and 0.015, respectively (Table 3).

Class-weighted logistic regression was retained as a sensitivity analysis because weighting is common for imbalanced outcomes but can distort probability calibration. Class weighting preserved similar discrimination but shifted mean predicted risks upward to 0.476, 0.440, and 0.459 in the three directions, producing much larger absolute calibration errors. We therefore used unweighted logistic regression as the primary probability model and interpreted class-weighted results as evidence that probability-scale conclusions are sensitive to modeling choices intended for classification rather than risk estimation.

Source-holdout analyses helped distinguish transport failure from source-model miscalibration. The unweighted MIMIC-IV ICU and eICU source models were close to calibrated on their own patient-level holdouts, with calibration slopes 1.047 and 1.043 and ECE 0.014 and 0.007, respectively. The NHANES temporal holdout contained only 17 one-year mortality events and had calibration slope 0.716, so the NHANES-to-MIMIC result should be read as a stress-test transport result under sparse source holdout events and endpoint-ascertainment differences rather than as a pure external covariate-shift effect.

### Calibration failure modes differed across scenarios

The strongest methodological signal came from calibration slope. NHANES-to-MIMIC produced marked slope distortion, with calibration slope 0.497 (95% CI 0.473-0.521) and intercept 0.307 (0.226 to 0.391). This pattern indicates that the probability scale was both displaced and distorted: updating the baseline risk alone would not repair the transported risk scale.

The ICU-to-ICU deployment scenario showed directional asymmetry. MIMIC-IV ICU-to-eICU had calibration slope 0.563 (0.519-0.606) and intercept -1.232 (-1.317 to -1.143), indicating moderate slope distortion plus baseline-risk displacement. By contrast, eICU-to-MIMIC-IV ICU had low absolute calibration error despite strong source separability, with ECE 0.016 and calibration slope 1.198 (1.050-1.338). Thus, the two ICU directions did not merely differ in shift magnitude; they occupied different calibration states.

Across these three directions, source separability alone was insufficient to choose a recalibration strategy. Both ICU databases were strongly separable by source-classifier AUC, yet MIMIC-IV ICU-to-eICU required slope-and-intercept updating whereas eICU-to-MIMIC-IV ICU was already close to calibrated on average. Calibration slope, intercept, and ECE together identified the operational state of the transported model more directly than source-classifier AUC alone.

### Local recalibration repaired calibration, but the best strategy depended on failure mode

Event-count recalibration quantified how many target-site outcome events were needed and which updating method matched each failure mode. In NHANES-to-MIMIC, the raw transported model had ECE 0.146 and calibration slope 0.497. Intercept-only recalibration reduced ECE to approximately 0.064 (95% empirical interval 0.059-0.068) with 100 local events but left the slope unchanged, confirming that baseline-risk adjustment alone was insufficient. Platt recalibration, which updates both intercept and slope, repaired the calibration-regression parameters: with 100 local events, ECE decreased to 0.018 (0.011-0.032) and slope increased to 1.041 (0.717-1.525); with 200 local events, ECE was 0.015 (0.011-0.025).

In MIMIC-IV ICU-to-eICU, Platt recalibration was again useful because the raw slope was moderately distorted. With 100 local eICU events, Platt recalibration reduced ECE from 0.059 to 0.012 (0.007-0.021) and moved the calibration slope toward 1.143 (0.824-1.568). With 200 local events, ECE was 0.011 (0.007-0.015). Intercept-only recalibration reduced ECE less completely in this direction, leaving the slope fixed at the raw value and ECE near 0.031 (0.031-0.033) with 100 local events.

In eICU-to-MIMIC-IV ICU, raw calibration was already close enough that local updating produced little improvement. Raw ECE was 0.016; intercept-only recalibration with 100 local events reduced ECE only slightly to 0.014 (0.014-0.015), and Platt recalibration yielded ECE 0.016 (0.006-0.033) with wider empirical intervals. This direction therefore illustrates a clinically important negative case: strong source separability does not necessarily imply that recalibration is needed. Isotonic recalibration reduced ECE in some settings but produced unstable calibration-regression parameters at low event counts. With 25-event isotonic updating, post-recalibration calibration-slope intervals were wide and far from the ideal value of 1: 0.093-0.940 for NHANES-to-MIMIC, 0.125-0.949 for MIMIC-IV ICU-to-eICU, and 0.139-0.969 for eICU-to-MIMIC-IV ICU. These results support isotonic recalibration as sensitivity analysis rather than as the preferred small-sample deployment method.

Decision-curve analysis evaluated whether recalibration changed utility for illustrative threshold-triggered actions such as escalation review, enhanced monitoring, or structured prognostic discussion. In NHANES-to-MIMIC, raw transport had positive but small net benefit at 20%, 25%, and 30% thresholds, whereas Platt or intercept-only recalibration with 100 local events improved net benefit across all three thresholds. In MIMIC-IV ICU-to-eICU, raw transport had negative net benefit at all three thresholds, and Platt recalibration converted net benefit to positive values. In eICU-to-MIMIC-IV ICU, raw transport already had positive net benefit at all three thresholds, with only small incremental gains after recalibration. Internal target-site histogram-gradient-boosting benchmarks remained higher, as expected when enough labeled target-site data are available to develop local models; recalibration remains relevant when only limited outcome labels are available or when an existing source model must be adapted rather than rebuilt.

### Subgroup patterns showed clinically important heterogeneity

Transport failure was not uniform across clinically important subgroups. Because ECE is prevalence-sensitive, these subgroup contrasts were interpreted as descriptive signals rather than definitive evidence of subgroup-specific calibration mechanisms. For NHANES-to-MIMIC, ECE was higher in older admissions than younger admissions (0.185 for age >=65 years versus 0.110 for age <65 years), higher in admissions with CKD than those without CKD (0.194 versus 0.124), and higher in admissions with CVD than those without CVD (0.167 versus 0.124). Similar but smaller patterns appeared in MIMIC-IV ICU-to-eICU transport, where ECE was 0.070 versus 0.047 by older versus younger age and 0.081 versus 0.055 by CKD history. In eICU-to-MIMIC-IV ICU, subgroup ECE values were generally low, although slope estimates varied across subgroups. These patterns support reporting clinically stratified calibration diagnostics rather than relying on average AUC alone.

## Discussion

This three-database study illustrates a diagnostic-repair approach to transportability failure rather than proving a universal rule. Across an extreme cross-setting stress test and a realistic ICU-to-ICU deployment scenario, transported diabetes mortality models retained moderate discrimination, while probability calibration depended on both transport direction and model specification. In the primary unweighted analysis, NHANES-to-MIMIC and MIMIC-IV ICU-to-eICU showed slope distortion that required slope-and-intercept updating, whereas eICU-to-MIMIC-IV ICU was close to calibrated despite strong source separability. These three cases support a practical diagnostic sequence rather than a single default recalibration rule: if target-site calibration diagnostics show baseline-risk displacement with a preserved slope, intercept-only updating may be sufficient; if the slope is distorted, Platt recalibration or slope-and-intercept updating is required; and if transported calibration is already acceptable, recalibration may add little. In practice, this diagnostic step requires only a small labeled sample from the target site to estimate the calibration slope before committing to a recalibration strategy.

This is the main practical message of the study. A transported model should not be accepted or rejected solely by AUC, and recalibration should not be chosen by habit. Instead, deployment should begin with a diagnostic sequence: quantify source shift, evaluate transported discrimination, estimate calibration intercept and slope, and then select the minimal recalibration strategy that matches the observed failure mode. Source classification remains useful for narrower tasks: it can identify measurable source-target separability, localize feature blocks that drive separability, and prompt external calibration assessment. The decision to update, however, should rest on the transported probability scale.

The distinction between discrimination and calibration was consistent across all directions. AUC remained in the moderate range after transport, suggesting that common clinical risk signals survived movement across data environments. However, probability calibration depended strongly on both transport setting and model specification. The class-weighted sensitivity analysis showed that weighting materially increased absolute risk displacement while leaving discrimination largely unchanged. This reinforces that deployment studies should evaluate probability calibration under modeling choices intended for risk estimation, not only for classification accuracy.

The event-count results provide a deployment-relevant scale for local updating. In the two slope-distorted directions, 100-event Platt updating reduced ECE substantially, but the empirical intervals remained wide enough to matter for deployment decisions; 200 events narrowed these intervals further. The 100-200 event range should therefore be interpreted as a practical scale observed in these data, not as a universal threshold. In prospective deployment, those events must be accrued through timely and reliable local outcome ascertainment, with governance for when updating is allowed and how label quality is checked. Outcome delays or inconsistent endpoint coding would increase the operational burden before recalibration could be trusted. This range is consistent with broad validation guidance emphasizing precision in the smallest outcome category, while reinforcing that sample-size needs depend on the recalibration model and the failure mode [26,27]. Intercept-only recalibration used one parameter and corrected baseline risk but could not repair slope distortion. Platt recalibration used two parameters and was needed when the slope was distorted. Isotonic regression was more flexible but unstable at limited event counts, making it less attractive as a primary strategy for small local calibration samples.

The eICU-to-MIMIC-IV ICU direction provides the strongest negative case in the study. Despite high ICU source separability, the transported unweighted model had low ECE and a calibration slope near the ideal range before updating. Although the calibration-slope point estimate of 1.198 was modestly above the ideal value of 1, the ECE was low (0.016), supporting a near-calibrated operational classification in this direction. This finding shows that strong source separability is neither necessary nor sufficient to determine whether recalibration is warranted. The decision point must be the transported probability scale itself, not source separability alone.

This contribution is related to, but distinct from, established model-updating frameworks. Prior work has defined when intercept updating, logistic recalibration, model revision, or more flexible updating may be appropriate [18,19,23]. Our added contribution is to connect that updating logic to real cross-database deployment: source separability diagnoses the presence and approximate character of shift, calibration slope and intercept diagnose the transported probability-scale failure, and event-count experiments give a practical scale for how much local outcome information is needed before choosing an updater.

The directional asymmetry in ICU transport may reflect the different structure of the two ICU databases. eICU is multicenter and may expose a broader mixture of institutional practices and measurement processes during source training, yielding a risk scale that transported reasonably well to the single-system MIMIC-IV ICU target in the unweighted analysis. In the opposite direction, a MIMIC-IV ICU-trained model was transported into a broader multicenter eICU case mix, where both baseline-risk displacement and slope distortion were more apparent. An alternative explanation is that direction-specific differences in feature missingness, first-24-hour measurement intensity, or hospital-mortality ascertainment shaped calibration independently of multicenter source diversity. These mechanisms are hypotheses rather than causal decompositions, but they are consistent with the observed difference between MIMIC-IV ICU-to-eICU and eICU-to-MIMIC-IV ICU calibration states.

The endpoint difference between scenarios is a design feature rather than a defect, but it remains an important constraint on interpretation. NHANES is a population survey with linked mortality follow-up, not an episode-based hospitalization database; it does not naturally define hospital mortality for an index admission. MIMIC-IV ICU and eICU, by contrast, both contain ICU and hospital discharge information that supports harmonized hospital mortality. Even within the NHANES-to-MIMIC stress test, one-year mortality ascertainment differed: NHANES used community mortality linkage, whereas MIMIC-IV used in-hospital death or post-discharge mortality after a hospital episode. Therefore, the observed slope distortion in this stress test may reflect both transport of a probability scale across settings and differences in endpoint ascertainment. The relative contribution of these two mechanisms cannot be separated in this design. We therefore did not intend the NHANES-to-MIMIC and MIMIC-IV ICU-eICU analyses to represent the same endpoint. They represent two complementary transport problems: an extreme population-to-hospital stress test using a longitudinal mortality endpoint, and a clinically realistic ICU-to-ICU deployment analysis using harmonized hospital mortality. Cross-scenario comparison should therefore focus on failure modes and recalibration behavior, not endpoint-equivalent absolute performance.

Several limitations should guide interpretation. First, these analyses are empirical case studies in one disease area and three data resources rather than evidence that the same recalibration behavior will appear in all clinical AI deployments. Second, NHANES, MIMIC-IV, and eICU differ in sampling frame, measurement process, outcome ascertainment, and clinical acuity; recalibration can repair probability scales but cannot remove all consequences of dataset shift. NHANES was also analyzed without survey weights, so the source model reflects the analytic NHANES sampling frame rather than a nationally representative diabetes population distribution. Third, the source-classifier findings came from two source contrasts and three transport directions, so they should be read as illustrative rather than as a quantitative rule linking separability to recalibration need. Fourth, the one-year endpoints in NHANES and MIMIC-IV were not identical: NHANES used linked all-cause mortality within 12 months, whereas MIMIC-IV used in-hospital death or post-discharge death within 365 days after discharge. Consequently, the NHANES-to-MIMIC calibration slope should be read as stress-test behavior under combined setting and endpoint-ascertainment shift rather than as a pure covariate-shift effect. Fifth, class weighting materially affected absolute probability levels in sensitivity analysis; this is why the primary analysis used unweighted logistic regression for probability estimation. Sixth, comorbidity definitions were harmonized pragmatically and may not be identical across survey-derived and diagnosis-code-derived sources. Seventh, subgroup ECE comparisons may partly reflect subgroup event-rate differences because ECE is prevalence-sensitive. Eighth, ECE depends on binning choices; equal-width 10-bin checks were reported alongside equal-frequency ECE, but calibration slope and curves remain essential complements. Ninth, the ICU analyses used first-24-hour laboratory and vital-sign summaries, which are clinically plausible but may still encode site-specific measurement practices. Tenth, recalibration samples were simulated from available target-site data. Prospective deployment would require reliable local outcome accrual and governance before recalibration could be used operationally.

Overall, the study illustrates a diagnostic-repair workflow for transported clinical AI. Source classifiers can identify the presence and approximate character of dataset shift; calibration slope and intercept can identify the failure mode of the transported probability scale; and small local outcome samples can guide recalibration strategy. Transport failure is therefore not simply a question of whether a model generalizes. It is a question of how the model failed, and whether the target site has enough local outcome information to repair the part of the model that failed.

## Methods

### Study design

We conducted a three-database transportability and recalibration study using adults with diabetes in NHANES, MIMIC-IV, and eICU. The analyses were organized into two complementary transport scenarios. The first was an extreme cross-setting stress test: a one-year mortality model trained in NHANES was transported to MIMIC-IV. The second was a realistic ICU deployment scenario: hospital mortality models were transported from MIMIC-IV ICU to eICU and from eICU to MIMIC-IV ICU. Logistic regression was the primary model class because calibration slope, calibration intercept, and intercept-only or slope-and-intercept recalibration are directly interpretable for deployment. Histogram-based gradient boosting was used as a benchmark model. Reporting was structured to follow TRIPOD+AI principles for prediction-model development, validation, calibration reporting, and transparent description of data sources [1].

Study size was determined by inclusion of all eligible diabetes records with known primary endpoint status in each source dataset and by fixed target-site holdout splits for the ICU transport analyses. No formal a priori sample-size calculation was performed for model development because the study evaluated transport and recalibration behavior using existing secondary data. Event counts for development, transport evaluation, and local recalibration experiments were reported explicitly, and the recalibration analysis was designed around prespecified local outcome-event targets of 25, 50, 100, and 200.

### Data sources

NHANES data were obtained from the processed 2005-2018 diabetes mortality cohort using public National Center for Health Statistics survey and linked mortality resources [28,29]. Participants were included if they had diabetes and ascertainable one-year mortality status. One-year mortality in NHANES was defined as all-cause death within 12 months among participants with known mortality follow-up. Participants who survived at least 12 months or died within 12 months were considered to have known one-year outcome status. NHANES analyses were conducted without survey weights because the objective was model transportability and recalibration rather than nationally representative mortality estimation.

MIMIC-IV data were derived from MIMIC-IV v3.1 [30,31]. For the NHANES-to-MIMIC stress test, the MIMIC-IV cohort contained diabetes hospital admissions with one-year mortality status defined as in-hospital death or post-discharge death within 365 days after discharge. For the ICU deployment scenario, MIMIC-IV ICU admissions were identified among diabetes hospital admissions with at least one ICU stay. When a hospital admission had multiple ICU stays, the first ICU stay by ICU admission time was retained. Hospital mortality was defined from `hospital_expire_flag`.

eICU data were derived from eICU Collaborative Research Database v2.0, a multicenter ICU database [32,33]. Diabetes candidate stays were identified using available diabetes indicators from APACHE variables, diagnosis tables, admission diagnosis text, and past medical history. Hospital mortality was defined as `hospitaldischargestatus == "Expired"`. Unit mortality was retained for sensitivity analysis but hospital mortality was used as the primary ICU-to-ICU endpoint because it was more clinically interpretable and more closely harmonized with MIMIC-IV hospital mortality.

### Endpoint alignment

Endpoint alignment was scenario specific. NHANES-to-MIMIC used one-year mortality because NHANES has longitudinal mortality follow-up but no index hospitalization from which hospital mortality could naturally be defined. MIMIC-IV ICU-to-eICU used hospital mortality because both ICU databases contain hospital discharge status. The one-year stress test also involved different ascertainment mechanisms across source and target: NHANES used linked community mortality follow-up, whereas MIMIC-IV used hospital-episode data plus post-discharge mortality. The Table 3 cross-scenario comparison therefore includes an explicit endpoint column. Absolute performance values should be interpreted within each endpoint-specific scenario; cross-scenario interpretation focuses on failure modes and recalibration behavior rather than endpoint-equivalent accuracy.

### Predictors and feature harmonization

For the NHANES-to-MIMIC stress test, the primary NHANES-compatible feature set included age, sex, BMI, systolic blood pressure, diastolic blood pressure, hypertension history, CKD history, and CVD history. Laboratory-augmented source-shift analyses additionally used available laboratory features such as HbA1c, glucose, creatinine, and lipids.

For MIMIC-IV ICU-to-eICU transport, the primary ICU-native feature set included age, sex, hypertension history, CKD history, CVD history, glucose, creatinine, BUN, WBC, hemoglobin, heart rate, systolic blood pressure, diastolic blood pressure, and SpO2. Laboratory and vital-sign variables were summarized from the first 24 hours after ICU admission. In MIMIC-IV these features were extracted from PostgreSQL-derived first-day ICU tables; in eICU they were extracted using ICU-relative offset times from 0 to 1440 minutes. Albumin was excluded from the primary model because both ICU databases had approximately 42% coverage, and temperature was excluded because eICU coverage was low. BMI was used in the NHANES-compatible restricted sensitivity set but not the ICU-native primary model because MIMIC-IV ICU BMI coverage was moderate.

Missing numeric predictors were imputed using median imputation within each fitted model pipeline, followed by standardization. Binary predictors were imputed with the most frequent value. No target-site outcome information was used to train the source model before transport.

### Source-shift assessment

We assessed source separability using source-classification models, motivated by prior work on clinical dataset shift, non-stationary health records, and site-specific transport failure [11-15]. For NHANES-MIMIC, balanced source-classification datasets were constructed by downsampling the larger source to the smaller source size. For MIMIC-IV ICU-eICU, samples were balanced to 25,906 observations per source. Logistic regression and random forest source classifiers were evaluated using stratified cross-validation. Source-classifier AUC was interpreted as a shift diagnostic rather than as a causal explanation or a predictor of recalibration difficulty: higher AUC indicates stronger separability of the observed feature distributions and measurement patterns, whereas calibration regression was used to diagnose probability-scale failure.

### Model development and transport evaluation

NHANES models were trained on diabetes participants from 2005-2006, 2007-2008, 2009-2010, and 2011-2012 cycles and internally tested on the 2013-2014 cycle. MIMIC-IV one-year mortality models used a deterministic split consistent with the prior NHANES-to-MIMIC analysis. For the ICU deployment scenario, MIMIC-IV ICU and eICU each used patient-level 75/25 development/holdout splits. Source models were trained on the source development split and evaluated on the target holdout split. Target-site internal models were used as contextual benchmarks.

Two model classes were evaluated: logistic regression and histogram-based gradient boosting. The primary logistic regression analysis was unweighted because the objective was probability estimation, calibration diagnosis, and recalibration rather than classification under class imbalance. Logistic regression used scikit-learn's default L2-penalized `lbfgs` implementation with standardized continuous predictors and no spline terms or other nonlinear feature transformations. Class-weighted logistic regression was retained as a sensitivity analysis because weighting can alter the absolute probability scale. Histogram-based gradient boosting used 250 boosting iterations, learning rate 0.05, maximum 31 leaf nodes, L2 regularization 0.1, and fixed random seeds. Models were implemented as scikit-learn pipelines containing imputation and standardization [34].

Performance metrics included ROC AUC, average precision, Brier score, mean predicted risk, observed event rate, expected calibration error, calibration slope, and calibration intercept [7,16,17,35]. Expected calibration error was calculated using 10 equal-frequency bins after sorting predictions by predicted risk; 10 equal-width bins were also reported as a binning robustness check. Calibration slope and intercept were estimated from the logistic calibration model:

```text
outcome ~ intercept + slope * logit(predicted probability)
```

Perfect calibration corresponds to intercept 0 and slope 1. Predicted probabilities were clipped to [1e-6, 1 - 1e-6] before logit transformation.

### Event-count recalibration

Local recalibration was simulated using fixed numbers of target-site outcome events. The primary cross-scenario analysis used event targets of 25, 50, 100, and 200 in all three transport directions. For each event target, the calibration sample was constructed by sampling the specified number of events without replacement and adding non-events so that the calibration sample approximately preserved the event rate of the target-site calibration pool. Recalibration was repeated 200 times per event target.

Three recalibration strategies were compared using established model-updating and probability-recalibration approaches [18-23]. Intercept-only recalibration estimated a single logit-scale offset while fixing the calibration slope at 1. Platt recalibration fitted a logistic regression model using the logit of the transported prediction as the sole predictor, updating both intercept and slope. Isotonic recalibration fitted a monotonic nonparametric mapping from raw predicted probability to observed outcome. Raw transported predictions were retained as the unrecalibrated reference.

For NHANES-to-MIMIC, calibration samples were drawn from the MIMIC-IV target evaluation data and excluded from that replicate's evaluation set before recalibrated metrics were calculated. This matched the original one-year mortality stress-test simulation, in which a separate MIMIC calibration pool was not reserved. Because the MIMIC-IV target evaluation cohort was large, with 6,297 events, exclusion of up to 200 calibration events had little effect on the event rate of the remaining evaluation set. For MIMIC-IV ICU-eICU, calibration samples were drawn from the target-site development split and recalibrated predictions were evaluated on the fixed target-site holdout split. This allowed the ICU deployment analysis to keep the transport holdout set separate from the local calibration pool.

### Decision-curve analysis

Clinical utility was evaluated with decision-curve analysis [36,37]. The selected thresholds were interpreted as illustrative risk cutoffs for triggering resource-intensive review rather than automatic treatment, such as ICU escalation review, enhanced monitoring, or structured prognostic and goals-of-care discussion. Net benefit was calculated as:

```text
net benefit = TP / n - FP / n * threshold / (1 - threshold)
```

Selected thresholds were 20%, 25%, and 30% for cross-scenario reporting. These thresholds were chosen as high-risk review cutoffs near or above the observed target event rates, where false-positive review burden becomes clinically relevant; they should be interpreted as sensitivity analyses, not as validated treatment thresholds. Strategies included treat-none, treat-all, raw transported logistic regression, intercept-only recalibration with 100 local outcome events, Platt recalibration with 100 local outcome events, and internal target-site benchmark models. The primary DCA contrast was raw transport versus simple recalibration strategies versus the internal histogram-gradient-boosting benchmark.

### Subgroup analysis

Subgroup transportability was assessed for age <65 versus >=65 years, CKD history, and CVD history. Within each subgroup, we reported sample size, event count, event rate, AUC, and ECE. These analyses were descriptive and intended to identify clinically important heterogeneity in transport failure rather than to perform multiplicity-adjusted subgroup inference. Because ECE is influenced by event prevalence and prediction distribution, subgroup ECE contrasts were interpreted as descriptive signals rather than definitive evidence of subgroup-specific calibration mechanisms.

### Statistical uncertainty

Transport performance metrics in the cross-scenario Table 3 were summarized with percentile 95% confidence intervals from 1,000 bootstrap resamples of the target evaluation cohort [38]. Recalibration uncertainty was summarized using empirical 2.5th to 97.5th percentile intervals across 200 repeated local calibration samples. Source-classifier results were reported as cross-validated AUCs. The completed TRIPOD+AI checklist will be submitted as Supplementary Information alongside the manuscript.

All analyses were conducted using Python with pandas, NumPy, and scikit-learn [34]. Analysis scripts and generated tables are stored under the project `scripts` and `outputs` directories to preserve a reproducible analysis trail.

The models were evaluated as retrospective research models and were not integrated into a clinical workflow or used to make patient-care decisions. Therefore, no user interface, real-time data ingestion process, alerting logic, or human-AI interaction protocol was implemented. Decision-curve analyses used illustrative high-risk review thresholds to evaluate potential clinical utility rather than to define a validated intervention threshold.
